# FlowState

**The first cycle tracker built for partners.**

Every period tracking app on the market -- Flo, Clue, Natural Cycles -- is designed for the person who menstruates. FlowState is designed for the person who loves them. It doesn't just tell you what day of the cycle it is. It teaches you what's happening inside her body, why she feels the way she does, and gives you real things you can do about it.

One input. One date. Daily guidance that actually makes you a better partner.

---

## The Problem

Most men have no idea what a menstrual cycle actually is. They know "period = bad mood" and that's about it. The reality is that a cycle is a 28-ish day hormonal symphony that affects energy, mood, sex drive, pain tolerance, sleep quality, appetite, skin, and emotional processing. Every single day is different.

Women know this because they live it. Partners don't, because nobody ever taught them. The information exists in medical textbooks and health blogs, but it's written for the person experiencing it, not for someone trying to understand and support them from the outside.

This gap creates a pattern: she feels off, he doesn't understand why, he says or does the wrong thing, she feels unsupported, he feels confused. Repeat monthly for the duration of the relationship.

FlowState closes that gap.

## How It Works

### Setup (30 seconds)

1. Enter the start date of her last period
2. Enter her average cycle length (default: 28 days if she's not sure)
3. That's it

### Daily Experience

Every day you open FlowState and get a **Daily Card**. The card has three layers:

**Layer 1: What's happening in her body right now**

Not vague hand-waving. Real biology, explained simply. For example:

> **Day 22 of 28 -- Mid-Luteal Phase**
>
> Progesterone is at its peak right now. Progesterone is a hormone that rises after ovulation to prepare the uterus for a potential pregnancy. Think of it as the body's "nesting hormone" -- it slows things down, conserves energy, and shifts focus inward.
>
> At this level, progesterone can cause bloating (the body retains more water), breast tenderness, fatigue (it literally has a sedative effect on the brain), increased appetite (especially for carbs and sugar -- the body is burning more calories in this phase), and heightened emotional sensitivity (she's processing feelings more deeply, not being "dramatic").
>
> Meanwhile, estrogen is dropping from its second smaller peak. Estrogen is the "feel-good" hormone -- it boosts serotonin and dopamine. As it falls, mood can dip. This is the same mechanism behind PMS. It's not psychological. It's biochemical.

**Layer 2: What she might be feeling today**

Translates the biology into human experience:

> - Energy is likely low. She may want to cancel plans or stay in.
> - She might feel more emotional than usual. Small things could feel bigger.
> - Cravings are real and physiological, not a lack of willpower.
> - She may feel less confident about her body due to bloating.
> - Sleep might be disrupted -- progesterone affects sleep architecture.

**Layer 3: What you can do about it**

Multiple concrete options, not just one generic suggestion:

> **Tonight you could:**
> - Cook her favorite comfort meal (her body is craving carbs for a reason)
> - Suggest a movie night instead of going out
> - Run her a bath with epsom salts (helps with bloating and muscle tension)
> - Bring her tea without being asked (chamomile or ginger are great right now)
> - Give her a foot rub or back massage (physical touch without pressure)
> - Simply ask "How are you feeling today?" and actually listen
>
> **Things to avoid:**
> - Don't suggest she's overreacting to something
> - Don't push for high-energy plans
> - Don't comment on food choices or appetite
> - Don't take emotional distance personally

## The Four Phases, Explained

The menstrual cycle has four distinct phases. FlowState covers each one in depth, every single day. Here's the full breakdown:

### Phase 1: Menstruation (Days 1-5)

**What's happening biologically:**

The uterine lining (endometrium) is shedding because pregnancy didn't occur. Progesterone and estrogen are both at their lowest point. The body is releasing prostaglandins -- chemical messengers that cause the uterus to contract to expel the lining. These same prostaglandins can affect the bowels (hence period-related digestive issues), cause headaches, and create inflammation throughout the body.

**Hormones at play:**
- **Estrogen**: Rock bottom. This means serotonin and dopamine are also low, which directly affects mood and motivation.
- **Progesterone**: Rock bottom. The sedative calming effect is gone, but so is the bloating and water retention (usually).
- **Prostaglandins**: High. These are the primary cause of cramps. They're not just in the uterus -- they circulate throughout the body, which is why period symptoms aren't limited to the abdomen.
- **Iron**: Dropping due to blood loss. This contributes to fatigue, especially with heavier flows.

**What she's experiencing:**
- Cramps that can range from mild discomfort to debilitating pain (some studies compare severe cramps to cardiac events)
- Fatigue and low energy from hormonal lows and iron loss
- Potential headaches or migraines (estrogen withdrawal is a migraine trigger)
- Lower back pain from uterine contractions radiating through the pelvis
- Possible nausea from prostaglandin activity
- Mood can actually *improve* partway through -- many women feel relief that PMS is over

**Partner actions:**
- Have painkillers (ibuprofen works best -- it's an anti-prostaglandin) easily accessible
- Hot water bottle or heating pad for cramps (heat relaxes uterine muscles)
- Make her favorite comfort food -- she's low on iron so red meat, spinach, or dark chocolate are legitimately helpful
- Offer to handle chores or errands she'd normally do
- Physical affection without sexual expectation (unless she initiates -- some women have higher sex drive during their period)
- Don't make it weird or act grossed out
- Stock the bathroom -- make sure pads/tampons/etc are always available without her having to ask

### Phase 2: Follicular Phase (Days 6-13)

**What's happening biologically:**

The pituitary gland in the brain releases FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone), which tells the ovaries to start developing follicles -- small fluid-filled sacs that each contain an egg. Usually one follicle becomes dominant. As these follicles grow, they produce increasing amounts of estrogen.

This rising estrogen is the reason this phase feels like a glow-up. Estrogen increases serotonin, dopamine, and endorphins. It improves skin quality, boosts verbal fluency, enhances memory, increases pain tolerance, and raises energy levels. It's the body's natural confidence booster.

**Hormones at play:**
- **Estrogen**: Rising steadily, getting higher every day. This is the main driver of the "feel good" window.
- **FSH**: Active, doing its job of developing follicles in the ovaries.
- **Testosterone**: Also rising slightly. Yes, women produce testosterone too -- it contributes to sex drive, motivation, and assertiveness.
- **Progesterone**: Still low. No sedative or bloating effects yet.

**What she's experiencing:**
- Energy climbing day by day
- Mood brightening -- she may feel optimistic, creative, social
- Increased sex drive (testosterone + estrogen combo)
- Better workouts -- strength and endurance peak in this phase
- Skin clearing up, may feel more attractive
- More adventurous, open to trying new things
- Better pain tolerance -- this is actually the best time for things like waxing or dental work

**Partner actions:**
- Plan active dates: hiking, gym together, trying a new restaurant, dancing
- This is your window for adventures, spontaneous plans, new experiences
- She's most receptive to big conversations or planning in this phase
- Initiate physical intimacy -- she's more likely to be in the mood
- Compliment her -- she's feeling herself and positive reinforcement lands well
- Suggest trying something new together (cooking class, road trip, whatever)
- Match her energy -- she's up, so be up with her

### Phase 3: Ovulation (Days 14-16)

**What's happening biologically:**

Estrogen hits its absolute peak, which triggers a surge of LH (Luteinizing Hormone) from the pituitary gland. This LH surge causes the dominant follicle to rupture and release a mature egg into the fallopian tube. This is ovulation. The egg is viable for about 12-24 hours.

This is the biological peak of the cycle. Estrogen is maxed out, testosterone hits its cycle high, and the body is essentially running its "attract a mate" program at full volume. Pheromone production increases, facial symmetry perception changes, voice pitch subtly rises, and even her scent changes.

**Hormones at play:**
- **Estrogen**: Peak. Maximum serotonin, dopamine, confidence, and social energy.
- **LH**: Surging to trigger egg release.
- **Testosterone**: Cycle high. Peak sex drive, assertiveness, and competitive energy.
- **Progesterone**: About to start rising but hasn't kicked in yet.

**What she's experiencing:**
- Peak energy, peak mood, peak confidence
- Highest sex drive of the entire cycle
- Most social and outgoing
- Skin is glowing, may look subtly different (studies show people rate women as most attractive during ovulation)
- Heightened senses -- smell, taste, and touch are more acute
- Some women feel mild pain on one side (mittelschmerz -- literally "middle pain" in German) as the egg releases
- Increased cervical mucus (clear, stretchy) -- this is normal and functional

**Partner actions:**
- This is your date night window. Plan something memorable.
- Physical intimacy is at its natural peak -- be present and attentive
- She's at her most communicative -- have meaningful conversations
- Take photos together -- she's feeling her best
- Be aware: if you're not trying to conceive, this is the fertility window. Contraception matters most right now.
- Match her social energy -- say yes to plans
- This phase is short (2-3 days), so don't waste it on Netflix if she wants to go out

### Phase 4: Luteal Phase (Days 17-28)

**What's happening biologically:**

After the egg is released, the empty follicle transforms into a structure called the corpus luteum (literally "yellow body"). This structure pumps out progesterone, which thickens the uterine lining in preparation for a potential pregnancy.

This phase is the longest and most complex. It has two halves:

**Early luteal (Days 17-21):** Progesterone rises alongside a second, smaller estrogen peak. Mood is still relatively stable. Energy is moderate. The body is in "wait and see" mode.

**Late luteal (Days 22-28):** If pregnancy doesn't occur, the corpus luteum starts breaking down. Progesterone and estrogen both plummet. This hormonal crash is what causes PMS (Premenstrual Syndrome). It's not in her head -- it's a measurable biochemical event that affects neurotransmitter systems, fluid balance, pain perception, and emotional regulation.

**Hormones at play:**
- **Progesterone**: Dominant hormone of this phase. Causes fatigue, bloating, breast tenderness, constipation, increased body temperature, and has a sedative effect.
- **Estrogen**: Has a second smaller peak around days 21-22, then falls. The fall is what triggers mood changes.
- **Serotonin**: Drops as estrogen drops. This is directly linked to mood changes, anxiety, and depression symptoms in PMS.
- **GABA**: Progesterone metabolites (allopregnanolone) affect GABA receptors, which is why some women feel anxious or on-edge -- the calming system is being disrupted.

**What she's experiencing:**

*Early luteal:*
- Relatively stable mood, maybe slightly more introspective
- Mild bloating may start
- Appetite increasing
- Energy is moderate but declining
- May prefer smaller social settings over big events

*Late luteal (PMS window):*
- Mood swings -- irritability, sadness, anxiety, or all three
- Bloating and water retention (can be 2-5 pounds of water weight)
- Breast tenderness
- Food cravings, especially sugar and carbs (the body burns 100-300 more calories per day in this phase -- cravings are the body asking for fuel)
- Acne breakouts (progesterone increases sebum production)
- Disrupted sleep (progesterone raises body temperature, making it harder to fall asleep)
- Headaches or migraines
- Difficulty concentrating
- Feeling overwhelmed by things that normally feel manageable
- Lower self-esteem or body image concerns

**Partner actions:**

*Early luteal:*
- Enjoy the calm before the storm. Low-key dates, cooking together, walks.
- Start being proactive about household stuff so she doesn't have to ask later.
- Deep conversations land well here -- she's reflective but not yet overwhelmed.

*Late luteal:*
- Patience is everything. She knows she's being more emotional. She doesn't need you to point it out.
- NEVER say "Are you PMSing?" Even if you're right, it invalidates what she's feeling.
- Stock comfort food. Don't comment on what or how much she eats.
- Offer physical comfort: blankets, heating pads, back rubs.
- Reduce friction. Handle logistics. Don't create new problems to solve.
- Validate feelings without trying to fix them. "That sounds really frustrating" > "Well have you tried..."
- Give her space if she wants it, presence if she needs it. Ask which one.
- Be patient with short tempers -- it will pass in a few days.
- Chocolate is legitimately helpful (magnesium + endorphin release).

## Hormone Glossary

FlowState doesn't just name-drop hormones -- it teaches you what each one actually does so you build real understanding over time.

| Hormone | What It Does | When It's High | What That Feels Like For Her |
|---------|-------------|----------------|------------------------------|
| **Estrogen** | The "feel-good" hormone. Boosts serotonin, dopamine, energy, skin quality, and cognitive function. Produced mainly by the ovaries. | Follicular phase, peaks at ovulation, smaller peak in luteal | Confident, energetic, social, sharp, glowing |
| **Progesterone** | The "nesting" hormone. Prepares the uterus for pregnancy, raises body temp, has sedative properties. Produced by the corpus luteum after ovulation. | Luteal phase (days 17-28) | Calm or fatigued, bloated, warmer, hungrier, sleepier |
| **Testosterone** | Not just a male hormone. Women produce it too. Drives sex drive, motivation, assertiveness, muscle function. Produced by ovaries and adrenal glands. | Peaks around ovulation | Higher sex drive, more assertive, competitive, energetic |
| **FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone)** | Stimulates follicle growth in the ovaries. Released by the pituitary gland in the brain. | Early follicular phase | No direct "feeling" -- works behind the scenes |
| **LH (Luteinizing Hormone)** | Triggers ovulation. Released by the pituitary gland. | Surges right before ovulation | Some women feel a twinge of pain (mittelschmerz) |
| **Prostaglandins** | Chemical messengers that cause uterine contractions. Also affect bowels, blood vessels, and inflammation. | During menstruation | Cramps, digestive issues, headaches, general achiness |
| **Serotonin** | Neurotransmitter that regulates mood, sleep, and appetite. Estrogen helps produce it. | Tracks with estrogen -- high in follicular/ovulation | Stable mood, good sleep, normal appetite |
| **Dopamine** | Neurotransmitter for motivation, pleasure, and reward. Also boosted by estrogen. | Tracks with estrogen | Motivated, pleasure from activities, sense of reward |

## Key Features

### Daily Cards
The core experience. Open the app, get today's card with biology + feelings + actions. Takes 60 seconds to read. Makes you 10x more thoughtful.

### Cycle Timeline
Visual timeline showing where she is in her cycle, which phase she's in, what hormones are doing, and what's coming next. Gives you the full picture at a glance.

### Action Library
Categorized suggestions organized by phase and situation. Meal ideas, date ideas, things to say, things to avoid, gift ideas, self-care suggestions you can set up for her. All tagged by cycle phase so the right ideas surface at the right time.

### Learn Mode
Short educational modules that go deep on one topic at a time. "What actually causes cramps," "Why cravings are real," "The estrogen-serotonin connection," "Why her sex drive changes." Each one is 2-3 minutes of reading that permanently levels up your understanding.

### Smart Reminders
Optional push notifications that are actually useful:
- "Her period is likely starting in 2 days. Good time to stock up on ibuprofen and chocolate."
- "She's entering her follicular phase. Energy is climbing -- plan something active this week."
- "Ovulation window. Date night?"
- "PMS window starts around Thursday. Keep the weekend low-key."

### Cycle Log
Simple way to refine accuracy over time. When her period actually starts, you tap "Period started today" and FlowState recalibrates. Over a few months, predictions get more accurate to her specific cycle length.

### Consent & Privacy
- The app should prompt you to let your partner know you're using it. Not as a secret surveillance tool.
- No data leaves the device. Everything is stored locally.
- Optional: she can connect her own tracking app so the data is shared, not guessed.
- No account required. No cloud sync by default.

## Why This Is Different

**Flo, Clue, Natural Cycles** -- these are built for the person who menstruates. They track symptoms, predict fertility, log data. They're medical tools.

**FlowState** is built for the person who loves them. It doesn't track symptoms. It doesn't predict fertility (unless you want it to). It teaches you biology and tells you what to do with that knowledge. It's a relationship tool.

The difference:
- Flo says: "Day 22. Luteal phase. Log your symptoms."
- FlowState says: "Day 22. Progesterone is peaking -- that's the hormone that makes her tired and bloated. She might be craving pasta tonight. Here are three things you could do."

## Technical Concept

### MVP Stack
- **Frontend**: React Native (iOS + Android) or start as a Progressive Web App
- **State**: Local-first. Cycle data stored on-device. No backend required for core features.
- **Content engine**: Phase/day mapped to curated content. Each day of a 28-day cycle has unique content across all three layers (biology, feelings, actions). Cycles of other lengths interpolate.
- **Notifications**: Local push notifications, no server needed.
- **Personalization**: Over time, users can mark which suggestions were helpful, and the app can prioritize similar ones.

### Data Model
```
Cycle {
  start_date: Date
  length: Int (default 28)
  periods: [PeriodLog] // actual start dates for calibration
}

DayCard {
  cycle_day: Int (1-28+)
  phase: Enum (menstruation, follicular, ovulation, luteal_early, luteal_late)
  biology: Text // what's happening hormonally
  feelings: [Text] // what she might experience
  do_this: [Action] // suggested actions with categories
  avoid_this: [Text] // things not to do
  hormone_levels: { estrogen, progesterone, testosterone, ... } // relative levels for visualization
}

Action {
  text: String
  category: Enum (food, date, physical_touch, words, logistics, gift)
  effort: Enum (low, medium, high)
}
```

### Content Scale
- 28 unique daily cards (one per cycle day) as the base content set
- ~5-8 actions per day across categories
- 12+ educational modules in Learn Mode
- Expandable: community-submitted actions, seasonal date ideas, etc.
